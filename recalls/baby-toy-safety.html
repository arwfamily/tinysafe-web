Toy recalls: choking, magnets & button batteries

The recalled toys parents actually search for — by hazard

The toy hazards that send kids to the ER are swallowed high-powered magnets and button batteries — plus choking on small parts. Before you buy a toy or add one to a registry, it's worth a quick recall check. TinySafe tracks 97 recalled baby and kids toys: 30 for magnet ingestion, 21 for button batteries, and 34 for choking. Swallowed magnets can pinch the intestines and button batteries can burn internally within hours — both are medical emergencies. Check a toy below before you buy, and confirm with the CPSC. What official guidance says
From the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC).
Small parts and choking (under 3)
Toys for children under 3 must not contain small parts that can be swallowed. A rough test: if a part fits inside a toilet-paper tube, it can be a choking hazard for a young child.
High-powered magnets are a medical emergency
If a child swallows two or more high-powered magnets, they can attract through the intestinal walls and cause serious injury. The CPSC restricts these magnets; seek emergency care if you suspect a child swallowed any. TinySafe tracks the recalls, not medical care.
Button batteries (Reese's Law)
Swallowed button or coin batteries can cause severe internal burns within hours. Under Reese’s Law, toys with these batteries must have child-resistant compartments. Keep batteries out of reach and seek emergency care immediately if you suspect a child swallowed one.
